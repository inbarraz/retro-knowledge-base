## Platform-Specific Knowledge

### Brazilian Apple II Clones (Overview)

#### Historical Context
- Brazil had thriving computer market in 1980s - Source: [YwVgmuYc708]
- Due to local laws, companies like Apple couldn't operate directly in Brazil - Source: [YwVgmuYc708]
- Local Brazilian companies cloned machines instead - Source: [YwVgmuYc708]
- Clones were legal in Brazil at the time - Source: [YwVgmuYc708]
- These clones are extremely rare outside Brazil - Source: [YwVgmuYc708]
- Brazil runs on 120V (like US) so power supplies should work - Source: [YwVgmuYc708]

#### Unitron Electronica
- Major Brazilian clone manufacturer - Source: [YwVgmuYc708]
- Made power supplies and motherboards - Source: [YwVgmuYc708]
- Associated with multiple clone brands - Source: [YwVgmuYc708]

### JEC 821A Apple II Clone

#### Overview
- Taiwanese Apple II clone by J.E. Computer Co., LTD, Taipei - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- Unusual case design resembling TRS-80 Model 1 - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- Uses 6502 processor like original Apple II - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- 48K RAM standard configuration - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- Also known as AMI III branding - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]

#### Power Supply Issues
- Power supply voltage may drift low over time - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- Symptom: System unstable or won't boot - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- Measured 4.3V instead of 5V on supply - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- Adjust internal potentiometer to restore proper 5V output - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- Check voltage with multimeter before diagnosing other issues - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]

#### Video Output Peculiarities
- Composite video output may have issues with some monitors - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- Requires proper 75 ohm termination on composite input - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- Without termination: washed out or unstable image - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- Some monitors have switchable termination (check settings) - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- CRT monitors with BNC input often have termination switch - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]

#### Keyboard Features
- Has lowercase support via shift key - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- Unusual for Apple II era (original Apple II+ had uppercase only) - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- Lowercase requires compatible software to display - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]

#### Compatibility
- Compatible with Apple II software - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- Uses standard Apple II expansion slot pinout - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]
- Disk II controller cards work - Source: [y-NpHq5CmA8]

### Macintosh Keyboard Cable (M0100)

#### Cable Polarity Danger
- Macintosh keyboard connector looks like telephone handset cable - Source: [TaHKjittUNI]
- Standard telephone handset cables have REVERSED polarity - Source: [TaHKjittUNI]
- Using wrong cable destroys keyboard controller inside keyboard - Source: [TaHKjittUNI]
- Reverses power polarity and kills the keyboard permanently - Source: [TaHKjittUNI]

#### Cable Identification
- Correct Mac cable: Black-Red-Green-Yellow on BOTH ends - Source: [TaHKjittUNI]
- Wrong cable (telephone): Black-Red-Green-Yellow vs Yellow-Green-Red-Black (flipped) - Source: [TaHKjittUNI]
- Cannot tell visually from outside - must check wire colors at connectors - Source: [TaHKjittUNI]
- Horrible design decision by Apple to use phone-style connector - Source: [TaHKjittUNI]

#### Compatible Machines
- K-Pro computers also use telephone-style connectors (non-polarized) - Source: [TaHKjittUNI]
- K-Pro cables work on K-Pro but destroy Mac keyboards - Source: [TaHKjittUNI]

### Apple II/II+ Power Supply (Astec)

#### Overview
- First personal computer to use switch mode power supply (1977) - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Unlike TRS-80 and PET which used linear supplies - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Made by Astec, very primitive design - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- No active power factor correction - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Works on Apple II, II+, IIe, IIgs - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]

#### Disassembly
- Some units have rivets on cover (drill out to service) - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Normal units have 8 screws only - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- 6 screws hold PCB to chassis - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Plastic nut/grommet on input cable - use Pros Kit CP311 tool - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Don't need to disconnect wires - can tip board out for service - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]

#### RIFA Capacitors
- Two RIFA X-caps on mains side - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- 0.01µF across hot/neutral (near fuse) - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- 0.1µF (second cap) - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Replace both with equivalent X-rated safety caps - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Cracked RIFA caps will eventually short and smoke - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Can short even when computer is OFF (across mains) - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]

#### IEC Connector Warning
- Some PSUs have RIFA inside the IEC power input connector - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Look for larger metal can or plastic housing - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Can let go smoke while computer turned off - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Chuck Hutchins video shows PET with this issue - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]

#### Controller Circuit
- Very primitive switch mode controller - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Uses magnetic coupler (not opto-isolator) - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Just a couple transistors - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Small 220µF 10V cap near power resistor (C bakes over time) - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- If PSU doesn't start or slow to start: replace this cap first - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Power resistors get very hot, bake nearby components - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]

#### Output Rails
- 5V main logic rail - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- 12V rail - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- -5V rail - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- -12V rail - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Test with multimeter on capacitors near outputs - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]

#### Capacitor Quality
- Nippon Chemicon caps - extremely robust - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Original caps from late 70s often still good - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- No evidence of leakage after 45+ years - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Modern cheap caps may not last as long - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Full recap NOT necessary if caps test good - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]

#### Testing
- Use ESR meter for in-circuit capacitor testing - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Check AC ripple on outputs (should be minimal) - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Inspect for burned components, cold solder joints - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Check output rectifier diodes for shorts - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Check main switching transistor (may need to lift to test) - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]

#### Troubleshooting
- Blown fuse: Check what caused it (shorted transistor?) - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Won't start: Replace small controller cap first - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- Slow to start: Same - controller cap - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]
- No output: Check switching transistor, passives, diodes - Source: [EJ0IKmHAfoA]

### Apple IIGS

#### Zip GSX Accelerator

##### Overview
- CPU accelerator card for Apple IIGS - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Made by Zip Technology - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Uses 65C816 processor (same as IIGS) at higher clock speeds - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Very rare and expensive today - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Alternative: Applied Engineering TransWarp GS (also rare/expensive) - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Modern FPGA accelerator exists but is expensive - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]

##### Hardware Details
- Standard version runs at 8MHz (32MHz crystal divided by 4) - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Some boards have 28.3MHz crystal (slight overclock) - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Original IIGS processor: ~2.8MHz - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- SRAM cache accelerates performance significantly - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Zip Chip Incorporated (1989-1991 date codes on ICs) - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Version 1.02 and 1.09 variants observed - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]

##### Installation
- Plugs into slot in IIGS - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Cable connects to original CPU socket - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Original 65C816 processor relocated to card - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Socket on card allows running at original speed when needed - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]

##### RAM Chips
- Original boards used 8K SRAM chips (70ns, slower) - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Upgraded boards use 32K SRAM chips (Cypress, 15ns, faster) - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- 32K chips common from 386/486 era cache memory - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Mod involves adding sockets and larger SRAM chips - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]

##### Configuration Jumpers
- Multiple jumpers on board for configuration - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Some boards have small wire mods - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- J8 jumper may or may not be installed depending on version - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- Clock crystal can be changed for overclocking - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]

##### Historical Notes
- Author had TransWarp GS as a kid, was expensive - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- TransWarp GS had cool 3D logo animation on boot - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- TransWarp GS could become unstable over time - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- 7-8MHz accelerated IIGS "absolutely flies" - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]
- 65C816 is efficient processor, faster clock makes big difference - Source: [eFatlDatjZM]

### Apple Color Monitor IIe (A2M2056)

#### Overview
- NTSC composite color monitor designed for Apple IIe - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Two manufacturers: Hitachi and Samsung (externally identical, different internals) - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Hitachi version identified by Hitachi CRT, more screws on bottom - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Samsung version has Samsung CRT and markings on PCB - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Fixed power cord, 120V NTSC only - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- PAL versions exist for 50Hz markets (Australia, UK) - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]

#### Physical Design
- Designed to sit on top of Apple IIe computer - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Has overhang/cutout at rear for Duo Disc drive to fit underneath - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Feet only under front section, rear hangs over drive - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Little door on front has metal hinge pins (Hitachi) or plastic flex hinges (Samsung) - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Plastic flex hinges on Samsung version break easily - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]

#### Controls
- Front: Power switch, contrast, brightness, color, hue, monochrome button - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Rear: Horizontal position, vertical position, vertical size, composite video input - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Drive/bias controls inside on flyback for color gun calibration - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]

#### Color Killer / High-Res Monochrome Mode
- Monochrome button switches to high-resolution monochrome mode - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- CRT has fine dot pitch for sharp 80-column text - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Unique feature: Color killer automatically switches to high-res monochrome when no color burst detected - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Unlike 1084/1702 which keep video filtering enabled even without color burst - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Results in sharp text instead of soft/blurry text on non-color signals - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Apple II 80-column text with artifact color is rainbow and unreadable - monochrome mode fixes this - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Apple Color Monitor IIc cosmetically different but internally same as IIe version - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]

#### Hot Chassis Design (SAFETY)
- NOT fully isolated - has "hot chassis" design - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- PCB has white line marking isolated vs live sections - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Ground on hot side referenced to B+ (mains potential) - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Third prong on power cord often broken off - DO NOT plug in wrong orientation - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Polarized plug required - larger prong is neutral, smaller is hot/live - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- If plugged in backwards or outlet miswired, chassis at 120V potential - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- MUST use isolation transformer for safe service - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- CRT ground wire connects to metal chassis (safe side) - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Degaussing coil runs around CRT perimeter - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]

#### Common Failure: Video Buffer Transistors
- Video signal path uses two transistors in emitter follower configuration - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- First transistor: C945 (NPN) - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Second transistor: A733 (PNP) - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Dead A733 transistor causes no video to reach processing IC - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Symptom: Horizontal sweep working (16.5kHz free-running instead of 15.7kHz locked), good raster, no image - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]
- Failed transistor may test shorted on all three legs - Source: [Ve5gsO4u7pc]

### Macintosh (Classic Series: 128K, 512K, Plus)

#### Mac 128K/512K Memory Architecture

##### Memory Map Overview
- 68000 boot process uses "overlay" mechanism - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- At reset, ROM appears at address $0 (where vectors are) - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- ROM also appears at $400000 (normal location) - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- After boot, overlay clears and RAM appears at $0 - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- PAL chip controls overlay bit based on VPA signal - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]

##### Memory Mirroring
- With 128K RAM, addresses mirror throughout lower memory space - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- Reading $20000 returns same as $0 (both point to same physical RAM) - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- Software can detect RAM size by checking for mirroring - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- Mac Plus/SE use SIMMs, different architecture than 128K/512K - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]

##### Video RAM Relationship
- Video display reads directly from main RAM - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- Display area is in upper portion of RAM space - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- Bad RAM in video area = garbage on screen - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- Bad RAM in lower area = crashes but may still show video - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]

#### Mac 512K RAM Troubleshooting

##### LS166 Shift Registers (Video Output)
- LS166 chips convert parallel RAM data to serial video stream - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- Eight LS166 chips, one per data bit - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- RQ (RAM output) lines connect directly to LS166 inputs - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- LS166 failure causes vertical lines/stripes in display - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- Video can work with dead CPU if LS166s and RAM are good - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]

##### LS244 Buffers (CPU RAM Access)
- LS244 buffers sit between RAM and CPU data bus - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- Required for CPU to read/write RAM - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- NOT required for video display (video has direct path) - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- Bad LS244 = display shows patterns but CPU crashes - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- Symptom: Sawtooth/checkerboard pattern, no Sad Mac - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]

##### Diagnostic Strategy
- If display shows recognizable pattern: LS166s and RAM likely OK - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- If no Sad Mac code: CPU can't read RAM (LS244 suspect) - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- Sad Mac with error code: Specific RAM chip failing - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
- Use logic analyzer on data bus to confirm CPU access - Source: [IPgH8YVVye0]
